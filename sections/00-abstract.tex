\abstract{


% Large language models are increasingly used during everyday work, yet it remains unclear whether these interactions function only as tools for task completion or also as sites of informal learning. We analyse 142,825 naturalistic human–LLM conversations from Microsoft Copilot and WildChat, focusing on coding and writing as two common task ecologies. Across 563,614 user turns, cognitive engagement appeared in 30.3\% of turns and constructive engagement in 4.9\%, while emotional engagement was rare. Learning-oriented engagement was higher in intentional than in unintentional interactions and higher in coding than in writing, but it remained detectable even when conversations were not explicitly framed as learning. Conversations containing scaffolding support were consistently associated with richer constructive participation and deeper post-answer interaction, with stronger associations in coding than in writing. However, support was heterogeneous: coding conversations were dominated by explanation-heavy support, whereas writing conversations were dominated by more directive support, and support forms differed in their associations with constructive uptake. Temporal analyses showed that the clearest signal was local and state-dependent. Scaffolded assistant turns were more likely than direct-response turns to be followed by constructive user turns in most settings, but longer-span within-conversation contrasts were mixed. These findings identify everyday human–LLM interaction as an underexamined site of informal learning, while showing that learning-oriented engagement depends on task ecology, support form and temporal scale rather than on scaffolding presence alone.

% General-purpose AI assistants are poised to become lifelong learning companions, yet it remains unclear whether everyday human--LLM interaction elicits the engagement through which people build understanding. We analyse 142,825 naturalistic Copilot and WildChat conversations, adapting learning-science constructs to over 1.1 million turns through human-verified LLM-assisted annotation. Across 563,614 user turns, cognitive engagement appeared in 30.3\% and constructive engagement---the stricter level in which users elaborate, test, revise or extend ideas---in 4.9\%. Constructive engagement was shaped by learning framing, task ecology and sustained exchange. On the assistant side, scaffolding was associated with richer constructive participation, but support form mattered more than scaffolding presence: feedback-like and explanatory forms aligned most clearly with constructive engagement. Scaffolded responses were more often followed by constructive user turns, whereas longer within-conversation contrasts were mixed. These findings make everyday AI-supported informal learning observable, providing a foundation for assistants that cultivate understanding rather than deliver answers.
